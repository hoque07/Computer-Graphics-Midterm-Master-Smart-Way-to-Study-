1) Introduction to Computer Graphics

Definition & Uses

Computer Graphics is using computers to create, manipulate and display pictures on screens or printers. Typical uses: CAD/CAM, GUI, education & training simulators, visualization, image processing, games, movies, VFX, advertising.

Key terms: pixel, frame/refresh buffer, refresh rate (Hz), resolution, aspect ratio.

2) Video Display Devices

CRT basics

  • Electron gun → focusing & deflection → phosphor screen.
  • Raster‑scan: scans rows top→bottom, 60–80 Hz using frame buffer.
  • Random‑scan: draws line segments directly (vector display).

Color CRT

  • Beam Penetration (random‑scan): limited colors (red, green, orange, yellow).
  • Shadow Mask (raster‑scan): 3 electron guns (RGB), wide color & realism.

DVST vs CRT

CRTDVST
Needs continuous refresh; flicker on dynamic imagesNo refresh; flicker‑free; image stored
Good for animationBest for static images; no partial erase

Flat Panels

Emissive: Plasma, LED, TFEL. Non‑emissive: LCD.

  • LED: Low power, long life.
  • LCD: Polarizers + liquid crystal + backlight; low power but limited viewing angle, fixed native resolution.

3) DDA Line Algorithm

Idea, Equations & Pseudocode

Step along the driving axis and increment the other by slope \(m=\Delta y/\Delta x\). Plot rounded coordinates.

  • If \(|m|\le 1\): \(x_{k+1}=x_k+1,\; y_{k+1}=y_k+m\)
  • Else: \(y_{k+1}=y_k+1,\; x_{k+1}=x_k+1/m\)
dx=x2-x1; dy=y2-y1
steps=max(|dx|,|dy|)
x_inc=dx/steps; y_inc=dy/steps
x=x1; y=y1
for i=0..steps: plot(round(x),round(y)); x+=x_inc; y+=y_inc
Cons: floating point + rounding → slower; may accumulate error. Prefer Bresenham for speed.

Solved Example (1 → 4, slope 2/3)

From (1,1) to (4,3): points ≈ (1,1), (2,1.67), (3,2.34), (4,3.01) → rounded: (1,1),(2,2),(3,2),(4,3).

4) Bresenham Line Algorithm

Derivation & Update

Choose between E=(x+1,y) and NE=(x+1,y+1) by a decision variable. Integer‑only arithmetic.

  • Init: \(p_0=2\Delta y-\Delta x\)
  • If \(p_k<0\): E → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2\Delta y\)
  • Else: NE → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2\Delta y-2\Delta x\)

Solved Example (9,18)→(14,22)

dx=5, dy=4, p0=3 → sequence: (9,18),(10,19),(11,19),(12,20),(13,21),(14,22).

Handle other octants by swapping x/y or mirroring signs.

DDA vs Bresenham

DDABresenham
Float + roundingInteger only (fast)
Conceptually simpleAccurate; industry standard

5) Midpoint Circle Algorithm

Key Ideas & Recurrence

Use circle function \(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2-r^2\) and the midpoint between E and SE to decide the next pixel in the first octant; mirror to 8 octants.

  • Init at (0,r): \(p_0=1-r\)
  • If \(p_k<0\): choose E → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2x_{k+1}+1\)
  • Else: choose SE → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2x_{k+1}+1-2y_{k+1}\)

Solved Example (r=10)

Start (0,10), p0=−9 → (1,10),(2,10),(3,9),(4,9),(5,8),(6,7),(7,7) stop when x>y; reflect to all octants.

6) Anti‑Aliasing (bonus/short)

What & Why

Aliasing = jagged stair‑steps on edges due to discrete pixels & insufficient sampling. Anti‑aliasing smooths edges.

  • Increase resolution (costly)
  • Area/weighted sampling (prefiltering)
  • Supersampling + post‑filtering

Rotate/scale about point (px,py): T(px,py)·R/S·T(−px,−py). Order matters!

Worked Minis

  • Translate (4,5) by (2,1) → (6,6)
  • Scale (2,3) by (2,3) → (4,9)
  • Rotate (4,0) by 90° → (0,4)
  • Reflect (x,y) about x‑axis → (x,−y)
