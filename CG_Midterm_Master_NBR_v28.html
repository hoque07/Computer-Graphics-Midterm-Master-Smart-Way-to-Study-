1) Introduction to Computer Graphics

Definition & Uses

Computer Graphics is using computers to create, manipulate and display pictures on screens or printers. Typical uses: CAD/CAM, GUI, education & training simulators, visualization, image processing, games, movies, VFX, advertising.

2) Video Disp

CRT basics

  • Electron gun → focusing & deflection → phosphor screen.
  • Raster‑scan: scans rows top→bottom, 60–80 Hz using frame buffer.
  • Random‑scan: draws line segments directly (vector display).

Color CRT

  • Beam Penetration (random‑scan): limited colors (red, green, orange, yellow).
  • Shadow Mask (raster‑scan): 3 electron guns (RGB), wide color & realism.

Flat Panels

Emissive: Plasma, LED, TFEL. Non‑emissive: LCD.

  • LED: Low power, long life.
  • LCD: Polarizers + liquid crystal + backlight; low power but limited viewing angle, fixed native resolution.

3) DDA Line Algorithm

Idea, Equations & Pseudocode

Step along the driving axis and increment the other by slope \(m=\Delta y/\Delta x\). Plot rounded coordinates.

  • If \(|m|\le 1\): \(x_{k+1}=x_k+1,\; y_{k+1}=y_k+m\)
  • Else: \(y_{k+1}=y_k+1,\; x_{k+1}=x_k+1/m\)
dx=x2-x1; dy=y2-y1
steps=max(|dx|,|dy|)
x_inc=dx/steps; y_inc=dy/steps
x=x1; y=y1
for i=0..steps: plot(round(x),round(y)); x+=x_inc; y+=y_inc
Floating‑point rounding Slower than Bresenham

Solved Example (1 → 4, slope 2/3)

From (1,1) to (4,3): points ≈ (1,1), (2,1.67), (3,2.34), (4,3.01) → rounded: (1,1),(2,2),(3,2),(4,3).

4) Bresenham Line Algorithm

Derivation & Update

Choose between E=(x+1,y) and NE=(x+1,y+1) by a decision variable. Integer‑only arithmetic.

  • Init: \(p_0=2\Delta y-\Delta x\)
  • If \(p_k<0\): E → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2\Delta y\)
  • Else: NE → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2\Delta y-2\Delta x\)

Solved Example (9,18)→(14,22)

dx=5, dy=4, p0=3 → sequence: (9,18),(10,19),(11,19),(12,20),(13,21),(14,22).

Swap x/y for steep slopes Reflect for negative slopes

5) Circle Algorithm

Key Ideas & Recurrence

Use circle function \(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2-r^2\) and the midpoint between E and SE to decide the next pixel in the first octant; mirror to 8 octants.

  • Init at (0,r): \(p_0=1-r\)
  • If \(p_k<0\): choose E → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2x_{k+1}+1\)
  • Else: choose SE → \(p_{k+1}=p_k+2x_{k+1}+1-2y_{k+1}\)

Solved Example (r=10)

Start (0,10), p0=−9 → (1,10),(2,10),(3,9),(4,9),(5,8),(6,7),(7,7) stop when x>y; reflect to all octants.

6) AA (bonus/short)

What & Why

Aliasing = jagged stair‑steps on edges due to discrete pixels & insufficient sampling. Anti‑aliasing smooths edges.

Increase resolution Area / pre‑filtering Supersampling + post‑filter

10) Previous Years (Questions & Model Answers)

Q1. Differentiate Raster‑scan and Random‑scan displays.

Ans: Raster: scans line‑by‑line using frame buffer; supports full color, good for images; may flicker. Random: draws specified line segments directly; high quality lines, limited color; suits vector graphics.

Q2. Explain DVST working with a neat sketch.

Ans: Uses a primary writing gun to charge storage mesh forming the image; a flood gun maintains visibility without refresh; high‑persistence phosphor → flicker‑free; cannot do partial erase; best for static images.

Q3. Using DDA, draw a line from (1,1) to (4,3).

Ans: dx=3, dy=2 → steps=3 → x_inc=1, y_inc=0.67. Rounded points: (1,1), (2,2), (3,2), (4,3).

Q4. Using Bresenham, draw a line from (2,2) to (9,6).

Ans: dx=7, dy=4 → p0=1. Plot: (2,2) NE→(3,3) E→(4,3) NE→(5,4) E→(6,4) NE→(7,5) E→(8,5) NE→(9,6).

Q5. Midpoint circle: r=8, center (0,0). Generate octant points.

Ans: p0=1−8=−7. Sequence (x,y): (0,8) E→(1,8) E→(2,8) E→(3,7) SE→(4,7) E→(5,6) SE→(6,6) SE→(7,5). Reflect to all octants.

Q6. Apply rotation by 30° then scaling (1.5, 1.5) about origin to point (2,0).

Ans: Rotate: (2cos30°, 2sin30°)=(1.732,1). Scale → (2.598,1.5).

Q7. What is anti‑aliasing? Mention two techniques.

Ans: Reducing jagged edges due to sampling; techniques: area/prefiltering and supersampling + post‑filtering.

Q8. Compare LED vs LCD.

Ans: LED emits light directly (via diodes), high brightness & efficiency; LCD modulates backlight using liquid crystals, low power but limited viewing angle and requires backlight.

Marking — Line Algorithms

What & Why

Examiners award full marks when your solutions show every decision step clearly. Present line/circle algorithms with explicit tables and correct transform composition.

  • Show every pk update in line/circle tables for full marks.
  • Rotate/scale about (px,py): T(px,py)·R/S·T(−px,−py) — order matters.
  • Write one step‑table from memory; take 5–10 min breaks between blocks.
